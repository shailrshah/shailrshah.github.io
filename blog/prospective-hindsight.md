# Prospective Hindsight: What Will Future Generations Look Down on Us for 100 Years from Now?

We look back on practices from the recent past with horror or confusion: human zoos, racial segregation, lack of women's suffrage. It is highly likely that people in 2125 will view some aspects of our world in a similarly critical light.

Predicting future moral frameworks is never exact, but historical patterns suggest that the most strongly condemned practices tend to be those causing systemic harm that societies normalize, abstract away, or outsource because they serve convenience or economic benefit.

Below are several candidates that may draw future condemnation.

1. **Industrial animal agriculture** — A future perspective may find it incomprehensible that billions of animals spent entire lifetimes in confinement and pain simply because they were not human, despite the existence of nutritional alternatives.
2. **Mass environmental degradation** — We treat ecosystems and the atmosphere as disposable utilities, even as data clearly indicates long-term consequences. Future generations might ask why ecological well-being was sacrificed for short-term economic metrics.
3. **Extreme wealth inequality amid global abundance** — It may seem morally indefensible that a minority held vast wealth while billions lacked basic necessities, especially in a world with ample resources and technological capacity for equitable distribution.
4. **Algorithmic exploitation and attention commodification** — If the future establishes cognitive privacy as a right, they may view our current acceptance of behavioral data extraction and attention manipulation as a profound violation of personal autonomy.
5. **Normalization of preventable human suffering** — From homelessness to untreated illness, many current social failures are implicitly accepted as inevitable or even deserved. This may later be regarded not as misfortune, but as ethical negligence.
6. **Healthcare as a market commodity** — A future with universal care may judge our system, where access to life-saving treatment is mediated by wealth or insurance status, as morally inverted.
7. **Work-centric identity and labor as survival prerequisite** — If economic structures evolve beyond mandatory wage labor for basic existence, future observers may question why people dedicated most of their waking lives to jobs that were not intrinsically meaningful simply to earn the right to live.
8. **Genetic selection and human biological stratification** — As gene editing and embryo selection expand, future societies may look back skeptically at the ethical boundaries we set, or failed to set, around altering human biology and potentially reinforcing inequality at the genetic level.

The common through-line is not ignorance. It is moral inconsistency: that people of our era often recognized harm yet still deferred to convenience, profit, or established social narratives.
